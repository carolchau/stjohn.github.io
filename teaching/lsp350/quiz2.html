Format

Quiz 2 consists of two parts:
  1. A multiple choice quiz on the reading, and
  2. A short essay based on the reading.
For Part 1, the Blackboard system randomly chooses from a pool of questions to make the quiz.  If you are not happy with your score, you can take the quiz over, and a new set of random questions will be generated.

For Part 2, you need to enter your essay answer to the chosen question.  Since there's several to choose from, make sure to clearly mark at the beginning which question you have chosen to answer.  Spelling and grammar do count, so, please use a spell-checker before entering your essay.  Since Blackboard will only accept plain text, it is probably easiest to write your essay in a word processor and then cut-and-paste it into the quiz window.

Topics

The questions in Part 1 are straightforward and based on the reading.  The best way to study for this is to do the reading.

For Part 2, choose one of the following questions and write a short essay.  Your essay should be about a page long and clearly address the question.  The questions below are from p xvi of the text:

  1. Samuel F.B. Morse transmitted over the telegraph, "What has God wrought?" He was quoting from the poem of Balaam in the Book of Numbers, chapter 23 (http://www.cforc.com/kjv/numbers/23.html).  Read the text of this poem.  The overview to this unit presents two ways to understand technical and scientific discoveries.  In which camp is Morse?  Explain your answer.
  2. In Walden, Thoreau remarked that "Our inventions are wont to be pretty toys, which distract our atttention from serions things.  They are but an improved means to an unimproved end, an end that it was already but too easy to arrive at... We are in great haste to construct a magnetic telegraph from Maine to Texas; but Main and Texas, it may be, have nothing important to communicate."  Substitute "internet" for "magnetic telegraph."  Do you agree or disagree with Thoreau?  Defend your answer.
  3. The invention of the computer is often compared in social impact to the invention of moveable type in the fifteenth century.  Do you agree?  Why or why not?
