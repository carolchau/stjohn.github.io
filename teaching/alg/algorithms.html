Suggested Syllabus for Analysis of Algorithms Core Course
     

This outline has been prepared for the Computer Science curriculum committee at the Graduate Center, Fall 2000.  This is just a suggested outline and syllabus and does not reflect the current policy of the Computer Science department at the Graduate Center.  For more information about the current course requirements, see the official bulletin at  Graduate Center.

The Analysis of Algorithms course is part of the core curriculum taken by all students in the doctoral program.  For many students, this will be the only formal course in algorithms they will take.  As such, the course content was selected to provide a basis in algorithm design and analysis and only an in-depth examination of a few special topics of the instructor's interest.  It is assumed that students wishing to study algorithms in depth will continue with more advanced courses in the area.
 

Prerequisites
    

The prerequisities for this course are the standard courses for an undergraduate degree in computer science.  The ACM has developed a guideline for the  computing curriculum, listing those courses.  Students are expected to have had basic algorithm analysis, algorithmic strategies, fundamental computing algorithms, and a minimum of one year's experience with a high level language such as Pascal, C, C++, or Java, familiarity with recursive programming methods and with data structures (arrays, pointers, stacks, queues, linked lists, binary trees).  In particular, students should know (list taken from the  ACM Computing Curriculum 2001):

AL1. Basic algorithmic analysis (core -- 4 hours)

  • Asymptotic analysis of upper and average complexity bounds
  • Identifying differences among best, average, and worst case behaviors
  • Big "O" and little "o" notation
  • Standard complexity classes
  • Time and space tradeoffs in algorithms
  • Using recurrence relations to analyze recursive algorithms
AL2. Algorithmic strategies (core -- 6 hours)
  • Brute-force algorithms
  • Greedy algorithms
  • Divide-and-conquer
  • Backtracking
  • Branch-and-bound
  • Heuristics
AL3. Fundamental computing algorithms (core -- 12 hours)
  • Simple numerical algorithms
  • Sequential and binary search algorithms
  • Quadratic sorting algorithms (selection, insertion)
  • O(N log N) sorting algorithms (Quicksort, heapsort, mergesort)
  • Hash tables, including collision-avoidance strategies
  • Binary search trees
  • Representations of graphs (adjacency list, adjacency matrix)
  • Depth- and breadth-first traversals
  • Shortest-path algorithms (Dijkstra's and Floyd's algorithms)
  • Transitive closure (Floyd's algorithm)
  • Minimum spanning tree (Prim's and Kruskal's algorithms)
  • Topological sort
  • Pattern matching and string/text algorithms
  • Numerical approximation algorithms

Syllabus
    

The course reviews and elaborates on the design of algorithms and their data structures.  The review material covers searching and sorting, and the more fundamental graph algorithms, with emphasis on correctness and efficiency.  Techniques for analyzing the complexity of algorithms are covered in depth, and include advanced topics on recurrence equations and NP-complete problems. Additional material includes advanced topics on randomized algorithms, amortized algorithms, and data structure design.

  • Review of recurrence relations, basic probability, and counting.
  • Sorting algorithms: mergesort, quicksort, heapsort, sorting in linear time.
  • Data structures: review of hash tables & binary search trees; red-black trees, disjoint sets, Fibonacci heaps.
  • Graph Algorithms: review of elementary graph algorithms, minimum spanning trees, and shortest paths; network flow, matchings, and dynamic graph algorithms.
  • Analysis and Design: amortized analysis, dynamic programming, linear programming.
  • NP-completeness and approximation algorithms for NP-complete problems, such as Steiner Trees, Traveling Salesman, and scheduling problems.
  • Introduction to randomized algorithms: analysis of quicksort, searching, and matrix multiplication.
  • On-line vs. off-line algorithms.
  • Programming practice.
The course also includes selected topics (the choice of the instructor) such as matrix operations, sorting networks, arithmetic circuits, string matching, algorithms for parallel computers, number-theoretic algorithms, and computational geometry.
 
 

References
    

The suggested text is Introduction to Algorithms by Corman, Leiserson, & Rivest (MIT Press 1990).

Students should know the following material before taking the course:

  • Chapters 1-6: Mathematical Foundations: growth of functions, recurrences, sets, relations, functions, graphs, trees, counting and probability.
  • Chapters 11-13: Data Structures: stacks, queues, linked lists, implementing pointers & objects, hash tables, binary search trees.
  • Chapter 17: Greedy Algorithms.
  • Chapters 23-25: Graph Algorithms: breadth-first and depth-first Searching, topological sort, minimum spanning trees, single-source shortest paths.
The material covered in the course includes:
  • Chapters 7-9: Sorting: heapsort, quicksort, and sorting in linear time.
  • Chapters 14, 20-22: Data Structures: red-black trees, heaps disjoint sets.
  • Chapters 26-27: Graph Algorithms: all-pairs shortest paths and maximum flow.
  • Chapters 16, 18, 25.5: Design Techniques: dynamic programming, amortized analysis, and linear programming.
  • Chapters 36-37: NP-Completeness & Approximation Algorithms:
  • Chapters 8, 13.4: Randomized Algorithms: analysis of quicksort, randomly built binary search trees, and examples included selected topics such as algorithms for parallel computers (Chapter 30), matrix multiplication (Chapter 31), primality testing and integer factorization (Chapter 33).
  • Chapters 22.4, 33.4: On-line vs. Off-line Algorithms: some material contained in disjoint sets and computational geomtry (supplement with material not found in text).
