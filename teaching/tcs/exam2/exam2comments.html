Comments for Exam 2

CSc 75010: Theoretical Computer Science
Graduate Center, City University of New York
Fall 2002


Grading

The exam was graded in the same style as the comprehensive exam in June.  Each question was worth 20 points.  The overal score was the sum of the best five question scores.  You can not score over 100%.

Regrading:

If you would like your exam regraded, please attach a note on the front of the exam.  Your note should explain which questions you would like regraded and why.  Please use complete sentences.  Do not write on the exam itself.  If you do so, your exam will not be considered.  You must turn the exam in for regrading by Friday, 13 December.

Scores:

After each exam was graded, the average of the different exams were calculated.  The scores were than normalized to make up for any differences in difficulty between the different versions of exams.  The average across all exams is 85 with standard deviation 13.

Comments

  • When taking the exam in June, each question must be answered on a separate piece of paper (actually, separate "blue book").
  • If you do better on the final, it will replace the either (or both) midterm exams.
  • Writing down the answer of another color exam not only gives you no credit but is cheating.

Question by question breakdown:


Question 1:  Define basic terms and build a simple Turing Machine.

Most everyone had no problems.  Note that to double a number in binary notation, you only need to "add an zero" to the end of the string.  A similar question to this has appeared frequently on the exam offered in June.  See for example, June 2002, #2.  On the June exam, this question is considered easy, so, mistakes on this are graded harshly.  The same was done for this exam.

Question 2:  Show sets are countable/uncountable.

These came straight from the homework and the book.  Again, similar questions appear frequently on the June exam and, since
considered easy, mistakes on this are graded harshly.

Question 3:  Show the Halting Problem plus some other set (varied by exam) is undecidable.

While each version of the exam had different questions, they were all variations on a theme.  Part a) was show the Halting Problem
undecidable.  Few showed this by the diagonalization method directly, but almost everyone did prove the statement.  Part b) was slightly harder and asked you to show some set was undecidable.  One way to do this, you reduce the halting set to the given set.  Some folks built the TM directly, others used the s-m-n theorem.  Quoting Rice's Theorem was given credit, but you should note that you will not get credit  for such an answer on the June Exam.


Question 4:  Easy questions about Turing recognizable and decidable sets.

The most common mistake was writing down the answer to a different exam.

Question 5:  State either Rice's Theorem and PCP.

Most got Rice's Theorem but forgot the proof.  For PCP, recall that you allow repetitions of the tiles.

Question 6:  Harder questions about Turing recognizable and decidable.

Hardest question was on the yellow and pink exams.  A good choice for J is 0A x 1B.
