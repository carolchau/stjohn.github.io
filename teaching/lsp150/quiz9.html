Format

Quiz 2 consists of two parts:
  1. A multiple choice quiz on the reading, and
  2. A short essay based on the reading.
For Part 1, the Blackboard system randomly chooses from a pool of questions to make the quiz.  If you are not happy with your score, you can take the quiz over, and a new set of random questions will be generated.

For Part 2, you need to enter your essay answer to the chosen question.  Since there's several to choose from, make sure to clearly mark at the beginning which question you have chosen to answer.  Spelling and grammar do count, so, please use a spell-checker before entering your essay.  Since Blackboard will only accept plain text, it is probably easiest to write your essay in a word processor and then cut-and-paste it into the quiz window.

Topics

The questions in Part 1 are straightforward and based on the reading.  The best way to study for this is to do the reading.

For Part 2, choose one of the following questions and write a short essay.  Your essay should be about a page long and clearly address the question.  The questions below are from p 198 of the text:

1. 2003 marks the thirtieth anniversary of the publication of an essay entitled "Animals, Men and Morals" by Peter Singer in the New York Review of Books.  This essay is often credited with beginning the animal rights movement.  Singer argues that because animals have feelings, they can suffer.  Because they can suffer, the have interests.  Because they have interests, it is, among other things, unethical to conduct experiments on them.  Suppose that scientists succeed in developing machines that feel pain and fear.  What obligations will we have towards them?  If this is difficult to imagine, watch the movie Blade Runner with Harrison Ford.  What do you think now?

2. The overview to this unit says that the dollar value of the output of the meat and poultry industries exceeds the dollar value of the output of the computer industry.  The overview mentions one reason why we hear so much more about software than chickens.  Can you think others?

3. Neither author, Ray Kurzweil or Michael Dertouzos, thinks that we should limit certain lines of research.  Do you?  Why or why not?
