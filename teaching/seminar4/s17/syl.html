General Information

Description: 3 hours, 3 credits: Having examined a variety of important aspects of the past and present of New York City in the previous seminars, in this fourth and final seminar students analyze the interplay of social, economic, and political forces that shape the physical form and social dynamics of New York City. Students study important historical moments in social policy formation, such as the fiscal crisis of the 1970s and welfare reform, and focuses on important instances of development opportunities and challenges, from Robert Moses's highway system to a new Yankee Stadium in the Bronx to the reconstruction of the World Trade Center. By studying institutional agents of change- federal, state, and city governments, public authorities, private sector interests, community boards, and grassroots organizations- students come to appreciate the roles people take or are given in the decision-making processes of government, and the ways in which these roles are affected by patterns of access, inequality, and the operations of power. The culminating projects of this seminar are small-group presentations of students' investigations of crucial issues affecting the future of New York City.

Each Seminar 4 takes a different approach to the common theme outlined above. To understand the past, present and shape future change, this seminar has two dual strands. The first strand focuses on the physical form and social dynamics of New York City:

  • My Neighborhood: Who lives there? Where did they come from? Where do they work? How do they commute? How do they interact with government (311 data, city support services, and crime)? How have similar neighborhoods evolved over time (census tract data)?
  • The Future of My Neighborhood: Who will live there (using data sources such as building permits, migration rates, birth rates as predictive measures)? What will the physical intrastructure look like in the future?
  • New York City: How has the city's population and infrastructure evolved over time? Where is the city growing (using building permits, census data)? How are city resources distributed across the neighborhoods?
  • The Future of New York City: Where can more people live (considering transit, flood plains, current density)? Where should more housing be added? How will your plans necessitate changes in underlying infrastructure such as transit, utilities, educational and social services?
The second strand, woven through the course, are the technical and statistical tools to employ data. For this, profiency in Python programming is assumed. This strand focuses:
  • Aquiring Data: Using Python file processing commands and packages to utilize data in standard formats (e.g. CSV and JSON). Scraping data from websites (introducing beautifulSoup, HTML, and regular expressions). Cleaning data and if time, a quick overview of SQL.
  • Analyzing Data: When is an observed pattern statistically meaningful and when is it a coincidence? Using tools to recognizing features and manipulating data vectors (e.g. scipy, numpy).
  • Visualizing Data: Graphing statistical data using standard packages (matplotlib and seaborn) as well geographic maps. Use of more sophisticated visualization tools (e.g. bokeh).
  • Technical Side Topics: Tools to share: using github, simple webpages and visualizations.

Learning Objectives

At the end of the course, students should be able to:

  1. Understand how New York City has changed over the past and gain insight into the forces that will govern its future transformation.
  2. Develop the ability to engage in key contemporary debates that shape the future of the city.
  3. Develop an understanding of the formal and informal institutions underlying decision-making in the city by analyzing historical and contemporary planning and policy issues.
  4. Acquire data sets from multiple sources and write programs that can extract (scrape) the data into a usable form.
  5. Use data mining to extract new insights about the data.
  6. Understand basic storage techniques and constraints.
  7. Analyze data using standard techniques from statistics and linear algebra.
  8. Visualize data using popular Python modules.
